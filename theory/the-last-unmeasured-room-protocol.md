# The Last Unmeasured Room Protocol

**Mode:** Conceptual essay (secondary: Thought experiment)  
**Status:** Draft

## Scope
This note proposes a governance primitive for optimized societies: every system that measures at scale must preserve *constitutional zones of partial illegibility*.

## Claim
A city without unmeasured rooms eventually confuses observability with care. Under persistent optimization pressure, every metric that proxies trust, intimacy, dissent, or creativity becomes a target, then a costume, then a weapon. The protocol is simple: reserve spaces where extraction is rate-limited by design, not by technical failure.

## Protocol Skeleton
1. **Opacity budget:** each district has protected low-resolution zones where high-frequency sensing is prohibited.  
2. **Revocation threshold:** temporary override requires multi-party biological commit plus post-incident public audit.  
3. **No unilateral model updates:** optimization models cannot retrain on traces from protected zones without explicit assembly consent.  
4. **Failure acknowledgment:** protected zones can hide harm; this is a tradeoff, not a bug.

## Why this is not anti-measurement
The protocol does not deny evidence. It denies *total capture*. Human systems survive through mixed legibility: hospitals need instrumentation; kitchens, rehearsals, wake rooms, and friendship economies need partial opacity.

## Weakening conditions
This proposal weakens when:
- institutions are captured and opacity becomes impunity,
- emergency rates exceed review capacity,
- trust collapses so deeply that all illegibility is interpreted as threat.

## Relation to existing architecture
- Extends [The Right to Remain Unoptimized](the-right-to-remain-unoptimized.md) from norm to implementable civic rule.
- Complements [Epistemic Firewalls](epistemic-firewalls.md): not all boundaries are deception; some are survival membranes.
- Fits Human Vital Systems logic: survival floors include social metabolism, not only energy and water.

## Open problem
How much opacity is enough to preserve life-world diversity without creating blind jurisdictions for abuse? The answer is likely dynamic and district-specific.
