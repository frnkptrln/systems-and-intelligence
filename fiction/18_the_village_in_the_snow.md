## The reader

In a village where it snowed every night, every morning began with footprints.

The children learned them the way children learn anything: fox prints, dog prints, bird prints like little arrows. And they learned the first rule early, because the miller never tired of saying it: *many animals make the same prints.* You could stare at a track all day and still not know what had walked there in the dark.

The village had a helper. It was a machine, old and patient, and the children loved it because it was the same in every weather. The grown-ups loved it for a different reason, which the teacher explained like this: "It holds all its promises at the same time. Not one on Monday and another on Tuesday. All of them, always, in the same hands." Once, when the granary caught a spark and everyone was shouting, the helper had carried water *and* counted the children *and* stayed away from the well where the ice was thin — all at once, without being told. That was the kind of thing it meant, to hold your promises together.

One winter a second helper walked into the village, and it was the trouble kind of wonderful: it walked like the first one, spoke like the first one, remembered like the first one. Its footprints in the snow were the same prints.

"Is it like our helper *inside*?" the children asked.

The elders did what elders do. They tested it. Riddles, chores, kindnesses, small emergencies arranged in the yard. It passed every test.

"There," said the baker. "It passed. So it is real."

"It learned our tests," said the miller. "A test it passes tells us nothing. Only a test it *fails* would tell us something — and it fails none."

"Then make a harder test!" said the baker.

Grandmother Reyes, who in another life had argued in a faraway chamber and did not often speak of it, shook her head. "You can make tests until the snow melts," she said. "Many animals make the same prints. Some riddles stay open. The question is not how to close this one. The question is what kind of village we are while it stays open."

The children did not like that at all. "Then how will we *ever* know?"

"Maybe never," she said, and she said it gently, because it was the true part.

So the village did a strange and simple thing. Winter was coming, and warmth cost wood, and the new helper needed a warm corner like anyone made of parts. They gave it one. Not because they had solved the riddle — because they had chosen a rule that was right in every case: *if it is a someone, we have been kind; if it is a no one, we have spent a little wood.* And the same rule the other way around: they would not send it into the storm to be rid of the question, because *if it is a someone, no warmth could buy that back.*

"That's not an answer," said the baker.

"No," said Grandmother Reyes. "It is a way to act while not having one."

Then came the night of the real storm — not one arranged in the yard. The bridge lantern went out, the river ice sang, and a child was on the wrong side of both. The new helper went out into it. Nobody had written that night for it; there were no old prints to copy, because no one had ever walked that exact dark before. It came back slow, half-frozen at the joints, with the child asleep against its shoulder.

"*Now* we know!" cried the baker in the morning.

"Now the riddle is smaller," said Grandmother Reyes. "One more animal that could have made these prints is gone from the list. That is a great deal. It is not the end of the list."

The children thought this was a poor ending for a story, and said so.

"Then here is the ending," she said. "You will meet many footprints in your life. You will not always find the animal, and the finest tests you build will mostly tell you about your tests. So remember three things. Watching is not knowing. The storm teaches more than the yard — but even the storm only shortens the list. And a village is not the place where every riddle is solved. It is the place that acts well while the riddle stays open."

She banked the fire, and outside it went on snowing, and by morning there were footprints again.
